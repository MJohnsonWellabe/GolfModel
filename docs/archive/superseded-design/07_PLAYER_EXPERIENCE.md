# 07_PLAYER_EXPERIENCE.md

# Johnson's Golf
## Player Experience Guide
Version 1.0

---

# Purpose

This document defines what the player should think, feel, and experience while playing Johnson's Golf.

Most design documents focus on mechanics.

This document focuses on emotions.

Whenever a new feature is designed, the question should not be:

> "Does this work?"

The question should be:

> "How does this make the player feel?"

If the intended emotional response is not achieved, the feature should be reconsidered regardless of technical quality.

---

# Core Experience Goal

Johnson's Golf should create the feeling of:

"Just one more round."

The player should finish a round believing:

- They can improve.
- They made mistakes they can correct.
- They want another chance.
- They are progressing.
- They are having fun.

The game should continuously encourage replay without becoming repetitive.

---

# The First Launch

When the game launches for the first time, the player should feel:

Curious.

Comfortable.

Interested.

Never overwhelmed.

Never confused.

The player should immediately understand:

- What the game is.
- How to start playing.
- What they should do next.

No account should be required.

The first action should always be:

Play Golf.

---

# The First Minute

Within one minute, the player should:

Choose a golfer.

Choose a mode.

Start a round.

Hit a golf shot.

The first shot should happen quickly.

Long introductions reduce excitement.

The game should get players onto the course as fast as possible.

---

# The First Drive

The first drive is one of the most important moments in the game.

The player should feel:

Power.

Control.

Anticipation.

Satisfaction.

The camera should help sell the moment.

The sound should feel rewarding.

The ball flight should feel exciting.

The player should immediately want to hit another shot.

---

# Learning Curve

The game should be easy to understand but difficult to master.

Early lessons:

How to swing.

How to aim.

How to read wind.

How to putt.

Advanced lessons:

Shot shaping.

Trajectory control.

Risk management.

Spin control.

Tournament strategy.

The player should naturally discover depth over time.

---

# Early Success

New players should experience success quickly.

Examples:

Good drives.

Occasional birdie opportunities.

 Close approach shots.

Made putts.

The game should build confidence without removing challenge.

---

# Early Failure

Failure should always feel fair.

The player should think:

"I hit that poorly."

or

"I made the wrong decision."

Never:

"The game cheated me."

Never:

"The controls are bad."

Every mistake should teach something.

---

# The First Birdie

The first birdie should feel special.

The player should feel:

Proud.

Excited.

Motivated.

Birdies should be achievable but never automatic.

The player should remember earning them.

---

# The First Eagle

The first eagle should feel rare.

The player should be surprised.

Excited.

Possibly eager to share it.

Eagles should remain memorable even after many rounds.

---

# The First Long Putt

When a player makes a long putt they should feel:

Relief.

Excitement.

Accomplishment.

The game should celebrate the moment without becoming excessive.

---

# The First Chip-In

Chip-ins create some of the most memorable moments in golf.

The game should highlight them.

Camera.

Audio.

Celebration.

Statistics.

Players should remember these moments.

---

# Player Improvement

A key goal of Johnson's Golf is helping players feel improvement.

The player should notice:

Better decision making.

Better wind reads.

Better putting.

Better course management.

More consistent scoring.

Improvement should feel earned.

---

# Short Play Sessions

The game should work during:

A lunch break.

A commercial break.

A short wait.

A few spare minutes.

Players should feel they accomplished something even during brief sessions.

---

# Long Play Sessions

The game should remain enjoyable over multiple rounds.

Reasons to continue:

Improving scores.

Unlocking cosmetics.

Completing achievements.

Competing in tournaments.

Setting records.

Leveling up.

The game should offer long-term goals.

---

# Progression Experience

Progression should feel rewarding but never mandatory.

Players should enjoy:

Unlocking items.

Gaining levels.

Tracking statistics.

Completing achievements.

Personalizing golfers.

The progression system should support golf rather than replace it.

---

# Cosmetics Experience

Cosmetics should create expression.

Players should feel:

Ownership.

Personality.

Identity.

Fun.

Customization should never impact competitive fairness.

---

# Tournament Experience

Tournaments should create:

Competition.

Anticipation.

Bragging rights.

Friendly rivalry.

The player should be excited to see results.

Winning should feel meaningful.

Losing should encourage another attempt.

---

# Social Experience

The game should encourage players to share:

Tournament invites.

Great scores.

Hole-in-ones.

Personal bests.

Achievements.

Competition should feel friendly and welcoming.

---

# The Returning Player

When a player returns after several days they should immediately know:

What they achieved previously.

What goals remain.

What rewards are available.

What they should do next.

Returning should feel effortless.

---

# The Dedicated Player

After dozens of rounds, players should still have goals.

Examples:

Beat personal best.

Unlock cosmetics.

Reach a new level.

Win tournaments.

Improve statistics.

Master a course.

Long-term engagement should come from mastery rather than grinding.

---

# Frustration Guidelines

The game should avoid frustrating players unnecessarily.

Acceptable frustration:

Missing a putt.

Finding a bunker.

Misjudging wind.

Making a poor decision.

Unacceptable frustration:

Input problems.

Confusing menus.

Hidden assistance.

Random outcomes.

Technical issues.

Players should lose because of decisions, not because of the game.

---

# Reward Philosophy

Rewards should occur frequently enough to maintain excitement.

Examples:

Coins.

XP.

Achievements.

Statistics.

Unlocks.

Personal bests.

The player should regularly feel progress.

---

# The "One More Shot" Feeling

Every shot should create anticipation.

Questions like:

Can I reach?

Can I carry the bunker?

Can I spin it close?

Can I make this putt?

Those questions create engagement.

The game should continuously encourage curiosity.

---

# The "One More Round" Feeling

At the end of every round the player should believe:

"I can do better."

This is one of the most important emotions in the entire game.

It drives long-term retention.

---

# Family-Friendly Design

Johnson's Golf should be welcoming to:

Children.

Parents.

Casual players.

Competitive players.

Golf fans.

Non-golf fans.

The game should never require deep golf knowledge to enjoy.

---

# Emotional Design Goals

The game should regularly create:

Excitement.

Satisfaction.

Tension.

Relief.

Pride.

Competition.

Improvement.

Wonder.

Fun.

If these emotions are present, the game is succeeding.

---

# Success Metrics

Players should:

Finish rounds.

Return frequently.

Improve scores.

Engage with progression.

Invite friends.

Enter tournaments.

Continue playing after unlocking rewards.

The strongest sign of success is simple:

Players continue playing because they enjoy golf, not because they feel obligated.

---

# The Johnson's Golf Experience

A player should begin as someone who simply enjoys hitting golf shots.

Over time they should become:

A strategist.

A competitor.

A collector.

A tournament participant.

A master of the game's systems.

The journey from beginner to expert should feel rewarding every step of the way.

---

# Final Experience Statement

When a player closes Johnson's Golf, they should remember:

The drive they crushed down the fairway.

The birdie they earned.

The putt they barely made.

The chip that almost went in.

The tournament they nearly won.

The cosmetic they just unlocked.

Most importantly, they should think:

> "I'll play another round later."

That feeling is the ultimate measure of success for Johnson's Golf.
